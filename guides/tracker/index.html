Why these four measures

  • Reviews place lean mass at roughly 15 to 40 percent of the weight lost on a GLP-1, so what the scale drops is not all fat.
  • Adding resistance training offset about 93.5 percent of diet-induced lean-mass loss in a meta-analysis, and protein up to about 1.6 g/kg supports it.
  • Because your body weight is falling, the scale cannot tell you if you are keeping muscle — strength over time is the home signal worth tracking.

The tracker does not invent targets or diagnose anything. It gives you a place to record the measures the research keeps returning to, and it is educational, not medical advice. A lasting drop in strength or function is a reason to talk to your clinician.

Frequently asked

What does the Muscle-on-GLP-1 Tracker track?

Four measures that, read together, show whether the weight you are losing is coming off the way you want: your daily protein in grams versus a target you set, the resistance-training sessions you complete each week, a simple weekly strength check on a few basic movements, and your body-weight trend over weeks. It is educational, not medical advice.

Is the tracker printable and reusable?

Yes. It is a 12-page PDF: a one-page guide to the four measures and how to set your targets, then ten undated weekly log pages. The pages are undated, so you can start any week and reprint as many as you need. It is laid out to print cleanly in black and white, with clear fill-in boxes and rules.

How do I set my protein target in the tracker?

The how-to page explains it. Most research on protecting muscle during weight loss supports roughly 1.6 to 2.2 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day, while a GLP-1-specific advisory from four medical societies recommends a more conservative 1.2 to 1.6 g/kg. You pick a number in that range, scale it to your body weight, and write it at the top of each weekly page. This is educational; discuss your own target with your clinician or a registered dietitian.
